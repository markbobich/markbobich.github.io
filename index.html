Dual Composite 3D Print Head
Senior Project

Working with the Composites Manufacturing & Simulation Center at Purdue University, I worked as part of a team of four on our senior design project to design a print head capable of printing continuous carbon fiber encased concentrically within discontinuous carbon fiber. The concept was that the continuous strands running within the discontinuous carbon fiber would create a stronger relationship much like rebar in concrete. This was a research based project, so while we did maintain a Bill of Materials, our goals were in the print head's functionality rather than its profitability. Our customer base was the potential industries that would benefit from new 3D composite technology, such as the aerospace, wind turbine, and automotive industries.

My role as CAD Lead required me to not only produce all of the CAD part files and drawings used for producing our prototypes, but also to conduct finite element analysis using Abaqus to confirm heater placements within the block. As a team we pitched concepts to one another and weighed the pros and cons of each design aspect while receiving input from our mentors at CMSC to arrive at our final design.



The print head would be connected to the Composites Additive Manufacturing Research Instrument (CAMRI) and was tasked with keeping the discontinuous carbon fiber at temperature, introducing and combining the continuous carbon fiber, being adjustable for multiple print diameters, and preventing backflow. Since this was a research based project and the first concept of its kind for carbon fiber printing, there was little data to work off of and that in turn lead us to design a print head with adjustable modular components to allow for various testing geometries.


Presentation Poster

We designed an experimental prototype, capable of testing the concepts we wanted to implement into our final prototype, but in a simple and smaller format. This prototype was used to confim the capability of our insertion tube concept for combining the two types of carbon fiber, the amount of backflow due to pressure, as well as our theory that the discontinuous carbon fiber would pull the strand of continuous carbon fiber once inserted.


Experimental Prototype

Some design elements incorperated into the final prototype include a modaular input adapter, continuous fiber instertion tube, and exit nozzle to allow for geometry adjustments. Carbon steel was used for all components since it had better thermal properties than standard steel or aluminum at the 300 C tempertures required to keep the carbon fiber molten, but was still able to be machined using the tools available to us in the machine shop.

The insertion tube required to insert the continuous carbon fiber also introduced a point for backflow which consequently could lead to jamming of the tube. The modular components allowed for pressure adjustments and the heater placements allowed for temperature gradient adjustments across the print head. These combined allowed us to minimize backflow by reducing pressure and temperature near the insertion point.


Abaqus FEA Temperature Gradient

The Deisgn for Manufacturing (DFM) skills I had learned in class helped me draft designs capable of being machined in the university's machine shop. I helped machine each part utilizing the lathe, mill, and CNC machines to produce both the experimental and final prototypes.
Part Drawing

Ultimately, the print head met the goals set for the project. The final prototype was able to produce samples that we could use to analyze the bonding of the materials. These samples showed promising results with cross sections that had no inconsistencies at the materials interaface and bonded the two materials together while meeting our diameter and print head compatibility requirements.

Improved Shower Caddy
Design Project

As a part of an engineering design course at Purdue University, I designed an improved shower caddy called the Shower Saver as a part of a team of four. The course concept was to design a marketable product in a market gap for a defined customer base.

The Shower Saver was marketed towards college students and adults who utilize public shower facilities. Our design incorperated a collapsable, lightweight stand that would keep toiletries off the wet and possibly dirty ground and allow for standing access. The stand had rubber feet to help keep the caddy stable and most parts were plastic to reduce cost.



My role on the team was to draft the arm linkages for the collapsable stand as well as the rubber feet. I also calculated forces associated with the design including stress and strain forces on the pins depending on diameter and the correlation between arm length and stand. As a team we vetted many ideas and utilized multiple design tools to narrow our design.


Presentation Poster

We utilized design tools such as functional decomposition and morphological charts to produce many design concepts and then inserted our favorites into a weighted decision matrix to analytically determine what concept was best.


Functional Decomposition Chart

Morphological Chart

Weighted Decision Matrix

Since our design had to be profitable, we created a Bill of Materials to keep track of the cost of every part and to get a total production cost. This production cost then would be used in our models to predict the breakeven point and future profit projections if we were to undergo mass production and sales of the Shower Saver. These findings were then compiled into a Net Worth Diagram.


Bill of Materials

Net Worth Diagram

In order to justify that the parts we bought would be sufficient, I calulated the stresses that the pins would experience and created a model the related the pin diamter to the maximum caddy weight capacity. A factor of safety of 2.5 was used and ultimately a pin size of diameter 2 mm was used that could safely handle 26 kg in the caddy.

Singapore Environmental Impact Analysis
Study Abroad Project

While studying at Nanyang Technological University in Singapore, I completed a basic Environmental Impact Assesment (EIA) of Singapore's liquified natural gas terminal as part of a team of five. The terminal had opened in 2010 and had recently added a 4th tank to its facility.

It was very beneficial to learn about how engineering is conducted in other countries. With how extensive Singapore's growth has been in the past decades, it enhanced my engineering knowledge to see how such modern advancements were being carried out. I believe it is through assements like the one my team and I carried out that has helped Singapore become such an advanced and extensive metropolis while maintaining its luscious ecosystem and greenery.




The process of an Environmental Impact Analysis incorperates four steps: screening, scoping, impact, and mitigaion. Screening is used to determine if an EIA is required and if it is, scoping is then used to identify areas of concern to increase the efficiency of the assesment. The impact analysis itself can be broken into identification, prediction, and evaluation, then mitigation measures are created for any impact deemed significant.


Environmental Impact Analysis Presentation

Our analysis found five potential environmental impacts: air quality, water quality, ecology, noise pollution, and cross border impact. Of these, ecology was deemed a significant impact and cross border impact deemed a slightly significant impact. This is due to pipelines on the seafloor disrupting the habitat and the fact that biocides used in plant operations are toxic to marine life. Since Singapore relies so heavily on imported natural gas, it impacts the cross border trade significantly, but this change is reversible, reducing its significance.

We determined that some mitgation measures should be undertaken for these impacts we deemed significant. The tanks must meet international safety standards and the terminal should be inspected regularly. The wastewater must be treated before release and pipelines should be built significantly beneath the seabed.

Overall we found the project suitable to be built and with the mitigation measures in place believe it to be an overall benefit to Singapore.
